The 2019 Yakima Skyline 50k

Posted on Sat 20 April 2019 in Trail Running • 8 min read

I normally don't write up trail reports. Normally I run, I have a good time, I finish, and that's about it. The stories are always about some personal victory or distance goal. This is not one of those stories. This is a story of trying to quit and being convinced to push on.

At this point in my trail running career I'm starting to see a lot of familiar faces. Rolling into camp the night before the 2019 Yakima Skyline 50k, I run into a few guys from the climbing gym. Not 5 minutes later some old trail running partners show up with beer. A text buzzes my phone notifying me that some other runners will be late. One of the race directors chats up our merry little band. Yup, we're excited and no one has really picked up the mileage since the Orcas Island races. Someone asks me about my Ragnar race from the previous weekend and how the lack of sleep has affected my training. At this point I realize that consecutive race weekends are probably not a great idea. Mistake number 1.

At this point I'm three beers in and thoroughly enjoying myself. Mistake number 2.

Let me guess, you were camping the night before? Yup. So, how many beers did you have? -Fellow runner with perfect hindsight

The following morning I'm changing into racing clothes, filling water bladders and making coffee. Other runners are milling about, getting ready and making morning chat. Feeling a little groggy, I get to the starting line a bit early. Considering the starting line is all of a couple hundred feet from camp, that's saying something. The final decision on racing clothes landed on 8" shorts, calf sleeves and a tank top. For shoes I decide against my road shoes and my oft-used S-Labs. Instead I go for a pair that I've only used a handful of times before. Spoiler alert, they're in the trash now. These shoes had given me problems during the Deception Pass 50k, but I chalked that up to poor training and an excess of road. I was wrong. Mistake number 3.

We're off at a fairly slow pace leading up to the first main climb. What everyone else knows that I don't (I signed up for this without reading anything. Mistake number 4) is that as soon as we start the first 2000ft climb, the group turns into a mile-long conga line of climbing on single track. I get locked into a crew of chatters spending the climb wise-cracking at each other and myself. We make it to the top together and cruise across the ridgeline to the first aid/water station. By now we're about 5ish miles in.

With a slight amount of shock, I come to the realization that I've only had a couple sips of water. Mistake number 5. A little over 2 miles of downhill later to aid station number 2 and this mistake repeats itself. Alright, Kevin, let's remember to stay hydrated. I know you've been training for races with less water, but this is supposed to make you more economical, not more negligent.

We come to the next big uphill and the rest of the group beats me to the top. I'm starting to slow down and really feel these hills. By the time I get to the next downhill I lose sight of the rest of the group and I start to walk a little. The footing is on loose talus and scree and making running difficult. Immediately I become envious of runners with trek poles. About 3 miles from the next aid station my water runs out. I'm in trouble.

Hey, remember that terrible footing I was talking about? Doesn't it suck right now? -Fellow runner

Rolling into the next aid station at the halfway point, I'm in rough condition. This race is an out-and-back so the second half is going to be the first half run in reverse. The second half is daunting. I ask about whether or not I should drop. I think I should. They think otherwise. I eat a ton of salty things and drink a ton of water and electrolytes. I probably consume more than I should have. Mistake number 6. Somehow, I forgot my salt tabs at home, and they didn't have any either. My feet are incredibly sore and I already hate my shoes, but I pull myself together and trot out of the aid station.

If I keep the same pace back, I should get in in just over 6 hours total. That's not going to happen.

About halfway up hill 3 I'm starting to hurt. My feet are killing me. The loose talus is bruising the insoles and the supposedly grippy rubber on my shoes is doing nothing. People start passing me. I slip from being in the top 50 to way back in the pack. A headache is starting to form, and suddenly I'm nauseous, but nothing is coming up. I start to walk more, but this has two immediate consequences. The first is that I'm running out of water FAST and the second is that cramps are starting to settle in. About a mile out of the aid station, I take the first of many breaks on the side of the totally-exposed trail under the beating sun. This is not looking good.

At the top of the first ridge, I'm looking at more uphill in the distance. Somehow, the cruising pace I took to get here in the first place had me overlooking how much I ran downhill to get here. I contemplate turning around, but I don't know if I can get back in time to get a ride to the start. The realization that I'm going to DNF (did-not-finish) this race is settling in. I've never DNF'd before. Everyone knows that it happens from time to time. Today is going to be my day, and the hit to morale is getting to me.

More passing racers. Now they're noticing that I'm in rough shape. They ask if I'm OK and I tell them not to worry; I'm DNF'ing. They take care of me, give me water and salt tabs. Some run ahead to the next aid station to tell them that I'm coming in slow and dehydrated. If it wasn't for these other racers, I would be in way worse condition. One of the other racers tells me I'm going to regret DNF'ing and to push on. I am so beyond caring right now. Everything hurts.

Some others catch up to me. They're also having a bad racing day. We roll into the next aid station. I'm sure I'm going to drop. I tell them as much, but they just tell me to sit down, drink some water and take a short break. The boat is coming in 30 minutes to pick up the other runners that are dropping so I have time to decide whether or not I am truly out of the race. There's about a half dozen other runners at this aid station. Most of them are dropping. We are having a bad day.

Well, you know the old saying. You run and when you can't run you walk. When you can't walk you crawl. And when you can't crawl; when you can't do that anymore... You find someone to carry you. -Firefly, I think?

My shoes are off, which is the fourth best thing to happen to me today. I pull them back on in anticipation of the boat. The others see me putting on shoes and a cheer rises from the volunteers and runners. A cowbell appears from out of nowhere. I think it was tied to the station pavilion all along. All right, guys, I'm going. I hate all of you (not really). I hit the trail again with only a few minutes before the boat shows up and everything hurts again.

Suddenly, a mile later, I feel OK. I can breathe. I can run. I mean, I can run. Uphill. The extra water and electrolytes must be kicking in so I pick up the pace. Some quick mental math yields that if I don't start averaging 15 minute miles on rough terrain, then I'm not going to make the cutoff. Alright Kevin, you got this. More passing, but this time, I'm the one passing others. The pace picks up. The next two miles on rolling terrain go by around 8 minutes apiece. This is going to work.

What hits me now is gratification. I few tears well up in my eyes that have nothing to do with the beating sun. Other people got me this far. Racers and volunteers. I am so happy to be surrounded by these people even if the closest one is a mile away. I choke it down in time to get to the last water station. This time I refill with some to spare, thank Doug (the guy at the last water station) and start running again.

The rest of the race passes by in a blur. The last downhill drained most of my remaining energy, but I didn't care. Other people got me this far, and I'm not letting them down. More running and I get to the finish line which sneaks up on me. The race director high-fives me and I begin to tell him about my day on trail. The full story will wait until after my feet go into the nearby creek.

The post-race party is full of people who helped me on trail. People see me, and ask about the rest of trail, or how I got back after the DNF. They're surprised when I tell them I made it. Hugs and high fives are passed around. Numbers are exchanged. I have a dozen new trail running friends and they're all great.

In a haze I grab a beer and some pizza. This all part of Rainshadow Racing's legendary post-race parties. The live bluegrass in the background is relaxing. I'm surrounded by people who are all doing the same thing. Moving through the crowds with the tell-tale limp, I can't go five feet without running into someone who saw me on trail. I catch up with the race director and we both share a good laugh about the day. I relive the worst parts on trail a few times, but now I'm just so happy the story has a good ending.

Of the 240(ish) racers who started the race, only 160(ish) finished. I'm happy to be one of them. It's my worst 50k time yet. Probably my worst running performance since before my first trail race. I don't think I had ever walked that much on trail before. I tried to quit. I've never tried to quit before. But I finished and that means the world to me. The best part was the people. Those people who kept me going and pushed me to finish. I don't think I'll ever think of quitting again. This is just as much about them as it is about me.

I love my trail running framily.
